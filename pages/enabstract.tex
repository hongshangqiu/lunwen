% English Abstract

With the continued evolution of cloud computing and microservice architectures, software operations fault diagnosis increasingly relies on joint interpretation of runtime data. Logs provide semantic clues about abnormal events, while monitoring metrics describe the temporal evolution of system states. Together they form a frequently used category of observability data in operations analysis. Although prior studies have discussed log analysis, metric monitoring, knowledge augmentation, and structured reasoning separately, a relatively focused validation path is still lacking for how logs and monitoring metrics should be collaboratively organized within a large-model-based diagnostic framework. When task-oriented input structuring is absent, large language models in software operations diagnosis may still produce outputs with insufficient evidence, unstable conclusions, and inconsistent presentation.

To address this issue, this thesis proposes a software operations fault diagnosis method based on large language models and collaborative analysis of observability data from logs and metrics. In this thesis, the term ``large models'' specifically refers to large language models, and the observability data under discussion mainly consists of logs and monitoring metrics. The proposed method is centered on an LLM-based diagnostic framework and treats the collaborative organization of logs and monitoring metrics as the primary diagnostic pipeline. Logs are organized through time-window filtering, request-level correlation, and anomaly summarization; monitoring metrics are organized through anomaly identification, trend abstraction, and textual representation. The two data sources are then unified into a shared analysis context. On this basis, retrieval augmentation is introduced only for knowledge-dependent scenarios, whereas structured reasoning is used to constrain analytical steps and result presentation.

At the implementation and evaluation level, a research validation prototype is built together with an experimental framework that combines controlled scenarios and supplementary public samples. The main experiment contains 21 controlled samples from 7 fault families and compares five settings under the same model condition: Metric-Only, Log-Only, Log+Metric, Log+Metric+RAG, and Log+Metric+RAG+Structured. Supplementary experiments on public log samples are further used to examine the behavior of structured output across datasets.

The results show that, under the controlled setting of this thesis, the collaborative organization of logs and monitoring metrics is the primary source of performance improvement, with the Log+Metric setting achieving a Top-1 root-cause accuracy of 76.2\%. When retrieval augmentation is added on top of collaborative input, Top-1 accuracy further rises to 85.7\%, and the gain is mainly concentrated in middleware- and database-related scenarios. Structured reasoning does not further improve Top-1 accuracy, but raises output compliance from 66.7\% to 95.2\% and makes key diagnostic evidence more explicit. These findings support the method design of a primary diagnostic pipeline complemented by auxiliary mechanisms, while also indicating that complex fault propagation scenarios and larger-scale real-world validation remain to be studied with richer observational evidence.
