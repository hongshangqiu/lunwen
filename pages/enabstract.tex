% English Abstract

With the widespread adoption of cloud computing and microservice architectures, software operations fault diagnosis increasingly depends on heterogeneous runtime evidence. Large language models have recently shown strong potential in text understanding, information summarization, and complex reasoning, which makes them promising for operations diagnosis. However, directly applying general-purpose large language models to this task still suffers from limited evidence grounding, strong dependence on domain knowledge, and unstable outputs. In practice, logs provide event-level semantics while monitoring metrics describe resource changes and performance trends, yet relying on only one of them is often insufficient for stable diagnosis. Therefore, it is meaningful to study how to organize logs and metrics for large language models and how to improve their diagnostic stability with knowledge enhancement and reasoning constraints.

To address this problem, this thesis proposes a software operations fault diagnosis method based on large language models and collaborative analysis of logs and monitoring metrics. The method takes a large-language-model-based diagnostic framework as the core. Logs are filtered and summarized with time windows and request correlation, while monitoring metrics are processed through anomaly detection and textual abstraction. The two sources are then organized into a unified analysis context as the core runtime input of the model. On top of this evidence layer, retrieval augmentation is introduced as a knowledge-injection mechanism for knowledge-dependent scenarios, and structured reasoning is introduced as a reasoning-control mechanism to improve result consistency and auditability.

To evaluate the proposed method, a research validation prototype is implemented and an experimental framework is constructed with controlled scenarios as the main evidence and public samples as supplementary evidence. The main experiment contains 21 controlled samples from 7 fault families and compares five settings under the same large language model diagnostic framework: Metric-Only, Log-Only, Log+Metric, Log+Metric+RAG, and Log+Metric+RAG+Structured. The Top-1 root-cause accuracy of the five settings is 38.1\%, 52.4\%, 76.2\%, 85.7\%, and 85.7\%, respectively. The results show that log-metric collaboration is the major evidence-organizing factor for improving large language model diagnosis, retrieval augmentation further benefits middleware- and database-related scenarios, and structured reasoning mainly improves output compliance, raising it from 66.7\% to 95.2\% while also making key evidence more explicit. Supplementary validation on public log samples shows similar gains in output consistency.

The results indicate that, in the controlled setting of this thesis, a technical route centered on a large-language-model-based diagnostic framework, supported by collaborative log-metric evidence, retrieval augmentation, and structured reasoning, can support software operations fault diagnosis more steadily. More complex fault propagation scenarios and larger-scale validation in real environments remain open issues for future work.
