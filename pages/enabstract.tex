% English Abstract

With the widespread adoption of cloud computing and microservice architectures, software operations fault diagnosis increasingly depends on heterogeneous runtime evidence. Large language models are effective at text understanding and summarization, but direct use in operations diagnosis still suffers from fragmented evidence input, insufficient domain knowledge, and unstable outputs. The core problem addressed in this thesis is how to organize logs and monitoring metrics into stable and traceable inputs for large language models.

To address this problem, this thesis proposes a software operations fault diagnosis method based on large language models and collaborative analysis of logs and monitoring metrics. The method is centered on a large-language-model-based diagnostic framework. Logs are organized through time-window filtering, request correlation, and anomaly summarization, while monitoring metrics are organized through anomaly identification and textual abstraction. The two evidence sources are then unified into a shared analysis context. On this basis, retrieval augmentation is used to inject domain knowledge for knowledge-dependent scenarios, and structured reasoning is used to constrain analysis steps and result expression.

To evaluate the proposed method, a research validation prototype is implemented and an experimental framework is constructed with controlled scenarios as the main evidence and public samples as supplementary evidence. The main experiment contains 21 controlled samples from 7 fault families and compares five settings under the same large language model diagnostic framework: Metric-Only, Log-Only, Log+Metric, Log+Metric+RAG, and Log+Metric+RAG+Structured. The Top-1 root-cause accuracy of the five settings is 38.1\%, 52.4\%, 76.2\%, 85.7\%, and 85.7\%, respectively. The results show that collaborative log-metric input is more effective than single-source input, retrieval augmentation mainly improves middleware- and database-related scenarios, and structured reasoning increases output compliance from 66.7\% to 95.2\%. These results indicate that the proposed method can support software operations fault diagnosis more steadily in the controlled setting of this thesis, while complex fault propagation scenarios and larger-scale real-world validation remain open for future work.
